Meet Naomi

Naomi Howard a 50-year-old constitutional defense attorney and lifelong Texan from Waco, is running for Mayor of Arlington to bring a fresh, problem-solving perspective to the city's most pressing issues: the city budget gap, the unchecked proliferation of rental properties, and the desperate need for effective public transportation. Though she has never held public office, her extensive legal career has uniquely prepared her to lead.

Throughout her career, Howard has demonstrated a commitment to justice and innovative problem-solving. She has successfully argued for striking down a state criminal statute as unconstitutional, litigated prisoner civil rights cases, and defended elected officials. This wide variety of cases fuels her creativity and compels her to think about new, systemic approaches to reform within the criminal justice system‚Äîa critical skill she will bring to city hall.

Beyond her practice, Howard has served on the board of the Innocence Project of Texas for five years and was recently elected as the board's Vice President, dedicated to overturning wrongful convictions. In her downtime, she enjoys hiking, cooking Japanese food, and unwinding with a Scandinavian crime drama on Netflix.

Howard believes her experience as an advocate who fights for fundamental rights and looks for creative, systemic solutions is exactly what Arlington needs to close the budget deficit, ensure balanced neighborhood development, and finally build a viable public transit system.

Howard runs her own solo constitutional defense practice in Austin, specializing in trial and appellate advocacy in the state and federal criminal justice systems. Before that, she was a litigator at the prestigious firm Rusty Hardin & Associates in Houston, where she handled a diverse caseload including state and federal criminal matters and administrative agency cases. Her legal acumen is fortified by her experience clerking for the Honorable Bert Richardson of the Texas Court of Criminal Appeals in Austin, an experience that gave her deep insight into the judicial process, particularly concerning the death penalty.
